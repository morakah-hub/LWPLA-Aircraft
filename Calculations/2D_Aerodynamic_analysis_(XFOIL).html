2D Aerodynamic Report — MH60 Airfoil

This report summarises the two-dimensional airfoil analysis for the LW-PLA flying wing aircraft. XFOIL 6.99 polars were computed at Reynolds numbers 200k, 300k, and 400k (Mach 0, Ncrit 9), covering the launch, cruise, and higher-speed flight regimes. The airfoil profile is traced from the actual MH60 coordinate file.

The section is analysed at three Reynolds numbers because the aircraft passes through distinct operating states. The low Reynolds regime dominates launch and initial climb. The middle regime represents normal cruise. The higher Reynolds regime reflects faster flight where the airfoil is more efficient and the drag bucket is deeper.

Lift
The lift curve is nearly identical across all three Reynolds numbers through the linear range. This means the wing responds predictably from launch to cruise. The zero-lift angle is slightly negative, as expected for a reflexed flying-wing section.
Efficiency
L/D rises substantially with Reynolds number — from 61.9 at 200k to 77.7 at 400k. The drag bucket is deep and centered around 5–7°, which aligns well with the cruise operating point.
Stall
CLmax occurs near 11.5–12° and the roll-off is gradual. This is desirable for a tailless aircraft — there is no sharp stall cliff, and the pitching moment remains controlled near the edge of the envelope.

04

Interpretation of the polar trends

Lift curve

The lift curve slope is approximately 0.104 per degree (5.96 per radian) at Re 300k, consistent across all three Reynolds numbers through the linear range of −3° to 10°. The zero-lift angle shifts slightly with Reynolds number — from −1.18° at 200k to −0.78° at 400k — because the boundary layer displacement thickness changes with Re. This shift is small and has negligible impact on trim.

Drag bucket and laminar bubble

The large drag difference between 200k and 400k in the cruise range (CDmin 0.00961 vs 0.00659) arises from the laminar separation bubble on the lower surface. At low Reynolds numbers the bubble is larger, causes more pressure drag, and delays transition. As Re rises the bubble shrinks, transition moves forward on the lower surface, and CD drops substantially. This is the dominant aerodynamic effect in this size class of aircraft.

Pitching moment

Cm at the cruise angle (3°) is approximately −0.014 at Re 200k and −0.013 at Re 400k. The variation is small and the trend is smooth. The moment goes slightly positive above 10–11° due to the reflex camber — exactly as intended for a tailless section — before the wing stalls. This means elevon trim requirements do not change significantly across the flight envelope.

Stall character

CLmax occurs at 11.5° (Re 300k) and 12° (Re 200k and 400k). The drop beyond CLmax is less than 5% over the next two degrees in all cases. This is a gentle, progressive stall, which is the correct behaviour for a small tailless aircraft. The wing will give adequate aerodynamic warning before full separation, and recovery is straightforward.

05

What the results mean for this aircraft

Launch — Re ≈ 180–220k
Higher drag
CDmin ≈ 0.0096. The drag penalty is real but acceptable for a hand-launched aircraft — the launch phase is brief and the wing transitions quickly into the cruise regime as speed builds. Peak L/D ≈ 62 is still adequate.
Cruise — Re ≈ 290–340k
Efficient
CDmin ≈ 0.0077, peak L/D ≈ 71. The cruise operating point is α ≈ 2–4°, CL ≈ 0.35–0.50. This places the aircraft in the lower end of the drag bucket, which is comfortable and allows slight altitude adjustment without penalty.
Fast flight — Re ≈ 420–480k
Deepest bucket
CDmin ≈ 0.0066, peak L/D ≈ 78. The drag bucket deepens and widens at high Re. Fast flight is both efficient and gives the largest stall margin (operating well below CLmax). Structurally, the 3D wing sees induced drag that will soften these 2D values.
Design conclusion: The MH60 is a practical choice for this tailless aircraft. The lift curve is well-behaved and Re-insensitive through the normal operating range. The drag penalty at launch is real but brief. The pitching moment is smooth and the stall is progressive. The main limitation of this 2D analysis is that it does not capture induced drag or spanwise effects — the 3D wing efficiency will be lower than these polar values suggest, particularly at lower speeds.
