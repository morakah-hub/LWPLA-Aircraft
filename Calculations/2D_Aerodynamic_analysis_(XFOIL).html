2D Aerodynamic Report for the MH60 Airfoil

This report summarizes the two dimensional airfoil analysis used to select, understand, and defend the wing section for the LW PLA aircraft. The study compares Reynolds numbers of 200k, 300k, and 400k, which cover launch, cruise, and higher speed flight. The airfoil drawing in the header is traced from the actual MH60 coordinate file.

The section is analyzed at three Reynolds numbers because the aircraft passes through distinct operating states. The low Reynolds regime dominates launch and initial climb. The middle regime represents normal cruise. The higher Reynolds regime reflects faster flight, where the airfoil is more efficient and the drag bucket is deeper.

Lift
The lift curve shows how much load the airfoil can generate for a given angle of attack. This matters because it sets cruise angle, stall margin, and the lift available during hand launch.
Efficiency
The lift over drag ratio determines how far and how quietly the aircraft can fly. In practice, the most useful operating window is not the peak point alone but the region around it.
Stall
Stall onset is gradual rather than abrupt, which is desirable for a small tailless aircraft. The curves do not collapse suddenly, so the airfoil should remain readable and controllable near the edge of the flight envelope.

04

Interpretation of the polar trends

Lift curve

The lift curve is almost the same for all three Reynolds numbers through the normal operating range. In practice this means the wing will respond to angle changes in a familiar way from launch to cruise. The zero lift angle remains slightly negative, which is normal for a reflexed section and helpful for pitch balance on a flying wing.

Drag bucket

Drag is the place where Reynolds number matters most. The 200k case carries more drag through the low and mid angle region because the boundary layer has a harder time staying attached. The 300k and 400k curves are cleaner and more efficient, which is why the aircraft should prefer cruise speeds above the hand launch regime.

Pitching moment

The pitching moment curves remain similar across all three cases. That is good news for a tailless aircraft because it means the elevon trim requirement does not change wildly with speed. A stable moment trend also makes the airfoil easier to document and easier to fly.

Stall behaviour

Maximum lift occurs around 11.5 to 12 degrees, and the drop beyond that point is gradual rather than violent. That is exactly the kind of stall a small flying wing needs because the aircraft can warn the pilot before it runs out of lift instead of simply snapping off the edge.

05

What the results mean for this aircraft

Launch
Higher drag
The aircraft will need a committed launch and a clean transition into climb. The low Reynolds regime is not the most efficient part of the envelope.
Cruise
Best compromise
Around the 300k region, the airfoil is more comfortable. Drag drops, lift remains stable, and trim changes stay modest.
Fast flight
Deep efficiency
The 400k case reaches the best efficiency, so once the aircraft is moving well it benefits from a cleaner aerodynamic state.
Design conclusion: The MH60 section behaves like a practical tailless airfoil for a student aircraft. It has a smooth lift response, a usable drag bucket, and a controlled pitching moment. The main penalty occurs at the lowest Reynolds numbers, which affects launch more than steady cruise. That is acceptable because the mission profile is not endurance gliding. The airfoil is better described as a section that rewards motion than one that rewards hovering.
