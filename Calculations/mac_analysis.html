Planform geometry

The aircraft is a tailless flying wing. The entire span (centre body and both outer panels) shares the same airfoil and angle of incidence, so the whole span generates lift and must all be included in the MAC calculation. Two vertical fins provide yaw stability only; they play no role in pitch.

Mean aerodynamic chord (MAC)

The MAC is a chord length, not a position. Think of it as the chord of a simpler rectangular wing that would behave aerodynamically identically to the real tapered wing (same lift, same pitching moment). It's used as the yardstick for everything else: CG limits are expressed as a percentage of MAC, pitching moment coefficients are normalised by MAC, and the aerodynamic centre is located at 25% of MAC.

The formula weights each chord station by its own chord squared — larger chords count more because they carry proportionally more lift and moment than small chords. Dividing by the wing area then gives the area-weighted average chord.

MAC definition — integrated over full semi-span (centreline to tip)
c̄ = (1/S) × ∫₀b/2 c(y)² dy
S = wing semi-span area  ·  c(y) = local chord at spanwise station y  ·  y measured from centreline

The semi-span is split into two zones with different chord behaviour. The centre body has a constant chord of 350 mm, so its contribution is computed analytically (a simple rectangle). The outer panel has a swept LE and cranked TE, so it is integrated numerically with 50,000 evenly-spaced stations. The cranked TE makes the closed-form taper formula inapplicable.

The numerical result for the outer panel is 259.24 mm, a 0.3% difference from the cranked TE. The full-surface MAC of 275.19 mm is higher because including the constant-chord centre body (which has the largest chord on the wing) pulls the weighted average up.

MAC (275.19 mm) is larger than the mean geometric chord (257.84 mm) because MAC weights each chord by its square, so the large root chord counts more than the small tip chord. For any tapered wing, MAC > MGC. An AR of 3.30 is low, typical of a fast, swept flying wing. A lower AR also means a gentler lift-curve slope, which reduces pitch sensitivity and is useful for a tailless design.
04

Wing aerodynamic centre (AC)

The aerodynamic centre (AC) is the chordwise point about which the pitching moment stays constant regardless of angle of attack. For subsonic flow it sits at approximately 25% of MAC from the leading edge of the MAC station.

This is distinct from the neutral point (NP), which is the aircraft-level equivalent accounting for every surface. For this tailless flying wing (no horizontal tail, vertical fins contribute yaw only) the aircraft NP is approximately equal to the wing AC. They are conceptually different but numerically close for this configuration.

First-flight CG recommendation

Why CG must be ahead of the AC: Imagine the aircraft is hit by a gust and the nose pitches up. The angle of attack increases, generating more lift. If the CG is forward of the AC, that extra lift acts behind the CG and pushes the nose back down, so the aircraft self-corrects. If the CG is at or behind the AC, the extra lift pitches the nose further up, making it worse. Since this aircraft has no horizontal tail to provide a correcting moment, the wing alone must do the job, which makes CG placement critical.

Static margin is how engineers quantify this. It's the gap between the CG and the AC, expressed as a fraction of MAC. A static margin of 10% means the CG is 10% of MAC (27.5 mm) ahead of the AC.

Static margin and CG derivation
SM = (xCG − xAC) / c̄
SM > 0 → stable  ·  SM = 0 → neutrally stable  ·  SM < 0 → unstable (diverges)

xCG = xAC + SM × c̄

SM = 15% (forward, more stable):
xCG,fwd = 245.51 + 0.15 × 275.19 = 286.79 mm from TE

SM = 10% (aft limit, less stable):
xCG,aft = 245.51 + 0.10 × 275.19 = 273.03 mm from TE

Tailless note: On a conventional tailed aircraft, the horizontal tail pushes the neutral point aft, giving a wider safe CG range. This aircraft has no tail, so the neutral point stays at the wing AC, narrowing the usable window. Start at SM = 15% (287 mm from TE) and move aft progressively once flight behaviour is confirmed. Always measure CG in the fully loaded, ready-to-fly condition.

Computation method

Geometry extracted from DXF using ezdxf. Centre body contribution is analytic (constant chord). Outer panel integrated numerically (trapezoidal, N = 50,000). All distances in mm.

# y = spanwise from centreline · chordwise measured from TE (TE = 0)

# ── Centre body (y = 0 → 55 mm, constant chord) ─────────
S_A     = 55 × 350        = 19,250 mm²
∫c²dy_A = 55 × 350²      = 6,737,500 mm³

# ── Outer panel (s = distance from outer panel root, 0 → 370 mm) ─
y_LE(s) = 350 − (105/370) × s

y_TE(s) = (26.86/91) × s                                       0 ≤ s ≤ 91
         26.86 + (97.24−26.86)/(339−91) × (s−91)              91 < s ≤ 339
         97.24 + (105−97.24)/(370−339) × (s−339)             339 < s ≤ 370

S_B     = ∫₀³⁷⁰ c(s) ds        → 90,329.75 mm²
∫c²ds_B = ∫₀³⁷⁰ c(s)² ds      → 23,417,499 mm³

# ── Full surface ─────────────────────────────────────────
S     = S_A + S_B               → 109,579.75 mm²
MAC   = (∫c²dy_A + ∫c²ds_B) / S  → 275.19 mm
ȳ     = ∫ y·c(y) dy / S         → 180.77 mm from centreline
x_AC  = y_LE(ȳ−55) − 0.25×MAC  → 245.51 mm from TE
